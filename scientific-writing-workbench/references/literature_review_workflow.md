# Literature Review Workflow

Use this reference when gathering evidence for a draft, a review article, or a revision pass.

## Order of operations

1. Inspect local material first:
   - existing manuscript drafts
   - note files
   - exported reference libraries
   - PDFs already on disk
   - figures, tables, and analysis outputs
2. Extract identifiers and candidate citations from local material.
3. Build the evidence map before expanding the search.
4. Use optional external lookup only to fill clear gaps, broaden coverage, or verify contested points.

## Evidence map format

Each claim or planned subsection should have a row like:

| claim_id | section | claim | support_type | support_items | notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| C1 | Introduction | Example claim | sourced | Smith2022; figure2.csv | Confirm effect size wording |

Support types:

- sourced: directly supported by cited literature or supplied data
- inference: interpretation drawn from cited or supplied material
- unverified: plausible but not yet supported by checked evidence

## Search strategy

- Start from the precise question, population, system, method, or outcome.
- Expand with synonyms only after the first pass.
- Track search queries and why records were included.
- Prefer primary studies for direct factual claims.
- Use reviews to orient the field, not to replace primary evidence where direct support matters.

## Source ranking

In general, prefer:

1. peer-reviewed primary research directly addressing the claim
2. authoritative datasets or registry records
3. systematic reviews or meta-analyses
4. narrative reviews or perspective pieces
5. preprints, conference abstracts, or non-peer-reviewed material when clearly labeled

## Conflict handling

When sources disagree:

- state the disagreement directly
- avoid summarizing the field as settled
- note whether discrepancies may arise from design, sample, measurement, or analysis differences

## Output expectations

For drafting tasks, produce:

- an evidence map
- a section outline
- a short note on evidence gaps or lookup needs

For literature review tasks, also produce:

- a documented search strategy
- inclusion and exclusion rationale
- a synthesis plan by theme or question
